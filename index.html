About

I am a first-year PhD student in the Department of Zoology and Wildlife Conservation at the University of Dar es Salaam, advised by Prof. Nema Mseru. My research focuses on the diversity, taxonomy, and conservation of nocturnal mammals in the montane forests of the Eastern Arc Mountains. I am especially interested in how field surveys, morphology, molecular data, and ecological observations can be combined to uncover overlooked lineages and clarify species boundaries.

Before beginning my PhD, I spent several years conducting field surveys in the Udzungwa Mountains. Working in poorly surveyed high-elevation forests, often with guidance from local communities in the Kilombero Valley, has shaped my interest in how biodiversity discovery and conservation depend on both careful field observation and integrative evidence.

Projects

Cyanopithecus scintillans — the Bluebits

My current research centers on Cyanopithecus scintillans gen. et sp. nov. (common name: Bluebits), a small nocturnal primate discovered in the upper Sanje corridor of the Udzungwa Mountains at approximately 2,300 m elevation. The species is provisionally placed within Cercopithecidae as sister to Miopithecus based on multilocus phylogenetic analysis of five loci, and is distinguished by paired bioluminescent dermal organs — representing the first documented case of constitutive visible bioluminescence in a mammal. The type series is deposited at the Natural History Museum, London.

The formal taxonomic description has been done under the supervision of Prof. Nema Mseru (University of Dar es Salaam) and Dr. Elias R. Holloway (Stony Brook University).

Eastern Arc Montane Mammal Surveys

I contribute to ongoing efforts to document the mammalian fauna of the Eastern Arc Mountains through camera-trap surveys, nocturnal transect monitoring, and specimen-based documentation, in collaboration with the Natural History Museum, London (NHMUK) and the Wellcome Sanger Institute, Cambridge (WSI).
